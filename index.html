I build tools that help programmers work faster and with less frustration. I try to focus on "scratching my own itch" because it makes the work more satisfying and means I build more intuitive tools. I find that working in close relationship with the users of my tools has similar benefits. This relational approach is especially well-suited to small teams and developer-tools companies, where I can directly engage with those wielding the tools I build.

In these relationships, I focus on understanding understand how developers or users work and what slows them down. I build smaller fixes while designing systemic changes to address present-day problems as well as deeper long-term issues. Sharing my work throughout helps me stay attuned to users' needs.

My work is informed by a long history of building productive public relationships with people, both inside and outside of software. I've been building software for people I know (both friends and colleagues) for five years, so I have plenty practice working out what they need and implementing it. For full project descriptions see my resume. Some examples are: the ngrok developer environment and associated tooling which I've maintained for the past three years and miscellaneous dev tools at repl.it and open work labs. I also built e-worm.club a few years ago. It's a social site just for my friends that's been through several iterations before I settled on something like a private, more user-friendly tilde.club. Building these tools has taught me a ton about working in the messy space in between people and software.

I owe much of my focus on building relationships to the four years I spent as a community organizer in Chicago. Community organizing focuses on identifying the root causes of community issues and bringing people together to fix them. In my software work I rely on the same kinds of high-trust relationships to identify the deeper causes of developer friction and build tools to address them.
