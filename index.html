I build tools that help programmers work faster and with less frustration. The best tools are built by people "scratching their own itch" because they possess unique insight into what the user needs. This sort of empathy is crucial to my work; with a deep understanding of developer needs, I can build tools that help maximize the performance of software companies' most expensive asset- developer time. This approach is especially well-suited to small teams and developer-tools companies. In these environments, deeply understanding your users' work is a competitive advantage that you can't afford to pass up.

My work is built on the following practices: I build relationships with developers to understand how they work and what slows them down. I propose solutions and develop quick fixes; continuously sharing my work helps me stay aligned with developer needs. These practices comprise a continuous cycle that drives my work.

As programmers, we often reach for software to solve our problems. But sometimes changing human practices, like encouraging people to use feature flags or to use traces instead of logs, solves issues more quickly than off-loading the problem entirely to software. I have found that most problems facing developers require both new tools and new practices.

My work is informed by a long history of building relationships with people to solve important problems, both inside and outside of software. I've been building software for people I know (both friends and colleagues) for four years, so I have plenty practice working out what they need and implementing it. Some examples are: the ngrok developer environment and associated tooling which I've maintained for the past year and miscellaneous dev tools at repl.it and open work labs. I also have been working on e-worm.club for the past few years. It's a social site built just for my friends that's been through several iterations before I settled on something like a private, more user-friendly tilde.club. Through building these tools, I learned how to understand my users and developed the technical knowledge to build tools to meet their needs.

I owe my relational skills to the four years I spent as a community organizer in Chicago. Community organizing is all about uncovering the root causes of community issues and helping people collaborate to fix them. I use the same skills of empathy, establishing trust, and building relationships to help solve the issues facing my fellow developers.
